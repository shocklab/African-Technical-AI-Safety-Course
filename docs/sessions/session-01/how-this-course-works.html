🎯 What we'll cover

This is a technical AI safety course for fourth-year and honours students in mathematics and applied mathematics. You understand what a neural network is and you can program in Python; you do not need prior exposure to AI safety. This sub-session explains the course's shape, how it's assessed, and how to get the most from it.

🗺️ The shape of the course

Twelve weeks, two sessions a week — twenty-four sessions in five parts. The spine runs from idealised, formal questions to messy empirical practice, then into your own research.

I · Foundations & the problem

What technical AI safety is; deep-learning and scaling foundations; the core alignment problem; the physical substrate (compute, energy, minerals).

II · Training & aligning

How a frontier model is actually built — pretraining, SFT, RLHF — and the methods meant to align it: RLAIF, Constitutional AI, scalable oversight.

III · Robust, controllable, evaluable

Adversarial robustness and jailbreaks; unlearning and the control agenda; evaluations and technical governance.

IV · Mechanistic interpretability

Opening the model up — features, circuits, superposition — with hands-on TransformerLens labs.

V · Research project

A scoped, Colab-feasible safety project: a short paper + code, presented and peer-reviewed.

💡 Why this order? Idealised → empirical

The first half gives you formal objects you can reason about — objectives, optimisers, proxies, agents. The second half confronts real systems, where those clean ideas meet noisy practice. For a maths cohort this sequencing plays to your strengths: we start where you are most at home.

🧵 Four through-lines

  • The safety–capabilities distinction. The organising idea of the whole field; we return to it constantly.
  • Idealised → empirical. Formal models of agency first, then the messy systems we actually have.
  • African technical AI safety. Not a single week — frontier models are measurably less safe in African languages, and compute/data sovereignty shape who can build and govern these systems. "Whose safety, whose risks, whose values?" runs throughout.
  • Intellectual honesty. The field disagrees with itself. We steelman the skeptics (Session 20) and hold advocacy and doubt together rather than picking a tribe.

📊 How you're assessed

Components (indicative weights)

ComponentWeightNotes
Weekly labs / problem sets25%The coding labs; completion + correctness, resubmission allowed.
Paper presentation + critiques15%Present one paper; submit short critiques before the relevant seminar.
Failure-mode analysis10%~1,500 words mid-course; an African/Global-South-context failure mode is one sanctioned track.
Research project40%Proposal + 6–8pp paper + code; itemised rubric, incl. a "positioning & context" mark.
Peer review of projects10%Anonymised reviews of two peers' final projects.

Every project carries a one-paragraph context statement — who it helps, whose risk it addresses, and how (if at all) it applies in an African context. "Not applicable, because…" is a perfectly good answer for a pure-theory project; the point is that you've thought about it.

⚙️ How we work, practically

Two sessions a week

Usually one concept/seminar session and one lab or working session. Read the assigned material before class — sessions assume it.

Labs run on Colab

Every graded lab is built to run on free or Pro Google Colab — no special hardware needed. A few GPU-heavy labs are marked optional/demo.
