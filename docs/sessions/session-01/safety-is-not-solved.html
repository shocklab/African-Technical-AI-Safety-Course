Week 1 • Session 1.4

🔓 Safety Isn't Solved — Especially Not for Us

One concrete result that anchors the whole course, and how to read this field critically

🎯 What we'll cover

Abstractions are easy to wave away. So we end Session 1 with a single, concrete, peer-reviewed result you can hold onto all term — one that shows the safety problem is both unsolved and unevenly distributed. Then a short, practical guide to reading this field without being taken in by either hype or dismissal.

🔓 The anchor: guardrails that fail in isiZulu

🌍 A safety property that depends on the language you speak

Frontier chat models are trained to refuse harmful requests. Yong, Menghini & Bach (2023) showed that simply translating an unsafe request into a low-resource language — isiZulu among them — slipped past GPT-4's guardrails on roughly 79% of a standard set of harmful behaviours, versus about 1% in English. The model's "safety" was, in effect, an English-language safety.

The paper won a best-paper award at a NeurIPS 2023 safety workshop. It is not an edge case or a stunt — it is a clean demonstration of a structural fact.

What it shows about safety

Safety is not a property a model simply "has". It is contingent — on the data, the evaluation, the language. A safeguard that holds in the conditions it was tested in can collapse in conditions it wasn't. That is the recurring shape of this whole course.

Why it's our problem

Safety training and evaluation are overwhelmingly English-centric, so the models are least safe in exactly the languages spoken across this continent. "African technical AI safety" is therefore not a diversity add-on — it is a frontier robustness-and-evaluation problem that happens to land on us first.

💡 Where this thread goes

We come back to this result directly: as a robustness lab in Session 9 (translate a refusal set into isiZulu/isiXhosa and measure the gap), as an evaluation project in Session 11, and as the spine of Session 18, "AI safety from the Global South". Hold the example; the course keeps cashing it out.

🔍 How to read this field

You will read a lot of papers, blog posts, and lab announcements this term. A few habits keep you from being misled — by enthusiasts or by sceptics.

A reader's checklist

  • Claim vs evidence. What exactly is being claimed, and what was actually measured? The gap between headline and result is where most overclaiming lives.
  • What's the eval? On which benchmark, which prompts, which models? A result is only as trustworthy as the evaluation behind it — and evals are often narrower than the claim (Session 11).
  • Who's the population? Whose data, whose preferences, whose language? The isiZulu result is what you find when you ask this question of "safety".
  • What would falsify it? A claim that nothing could disconfirm isn't science. Look for the conditions under which the authors would have been wrong.
  • Cui bono / who's publishing? A lab's own safety report is evidence, but not disinterested evidence. Note the incentives without assuming bad faith.

💡 The reading landscape

Across the term we draw on Hendrycks' Intro to ML Safety (the technical backbone), BlueDot's curriculum (framing), Grosse's Toronto course (sequencing), and ARENA + Neel Nanda's materials (the hands-on interpretability labs) — plus the African ecosystem: Masakhane, Lelapa AI, the ILINA programme, and Research ICT Africa. You'll meet these as they become relevant; none is a prerequisite.

🧾 Session 1 summary & what's next

Technical AI safety separates misuse, misalignment and systemic risk; its organising idea is the gap between rising capabilities and lagging safety; the case for it is a chain of inspectable claims with serious critics on the other side; and the isiZulu jailbreak makes the whole thing concrete — safety is unsolved, and unevenly so. Read critically, hold beliefs at the confidence the evidence supports.
