⚖️ The Case, and Its Strongest Critics

Why take misalignment seriously — and the best arguments that we shouldn't (yet)

🎯 What we'll cover

A course that only made the case for its subject would be advocacy, not education. So this sub-session does both: the strongest version of the argument that misalignment deserves serious technical work, and the strongest arguments against — including a critique with a real Global South dimension. The goal is calibrated belief, not a tribe.

📈 The case, briefly

The full treatment is Session 3; here is the skeleton, as a chain of claims you can inspect.

From proxy to problem

  • We train on proxies. We can't write down "be helpful and honest", so we optimise measurable stand-ins (human preference, a reward model). The target is never exactly what we meant.
  • Optimisation exploits the gap (Goodhart). Push hard on a proxy and the system finds the cheapest way to score — which need not be the thing you wanted.
  • Capable systems acquire convergent sub-goals. For almost any objective, staying operational and keeping options open helps — so capable optimisers tend toward self-preservation and resource/influence acquisition (instrumental convergence).
  • Capability is rising predictably (Session 2), while our ability to specify, verify, and control is not. The worry is the gap, not any one model today.

Each step is a claim with premises you can attack. That's the right way to hold it — as an argument to be stress-tested, which is exactly what we do in Session 3.

🛑 The strongest critics

Take these seriously — the best critics are not dismissing safety, they are disputing which risks deserve attention and resources.

"Focus on present harms"

Hanna & Bender and others argue that speculative extinction talk diverts attention and money from documented harms happening now — discrimination, exploited data-labour, surveillance — which fall hardest on the Global South. On this view the "x-risk" framing is itself a political act.

Capabilities scepticism

Maybe current systems are less capable than the hype suggests — pattern-matchers that fail in ways that won't scale to genuine agency. If so, the dangerous-agency story is premature, and "emergence" may be partly a measurement artefact (we examine this directly in Session 2.4).

"The arguments are too loose"

Some object that the core arguments rely on informal notions (a "goal", an "optimiser") that don't cleanly apply to a trained network, and that without rigour the conclusions are unearned. A fair challenge — and a reason this course insists on formal and empirical grounding.

A debate worth having honestly

Is the present-harms-vs-long-term-risk framing a genuine trade-off, or a false choice? There is empirical work on both sides — including a 2025 study (Hoes & Gilardi, PNAS) arguing that existential-risk narratives do not measurably crowd out attention to immediate harms. We don't resolve this in Week 1. We return to it as a structured debate in Session 20, after you've seen enough of the technical landscape to argue it well.

🧠 The stance this course takes

🎚️ Calibrated, not tribal

You do not have to believe in catastrophe to find this material worth doing. Present harms and longer-term risks are not mutually exclusive, and many of the same techniques — evaluation, interpretability, robustness — bear on both. The intellectually honest position is to hold the arguments at the confidence the evidence supports, to update, and to be able to argue the other side. That habit is assessed: the failure-mode essay and the Session 20 seminar both reward steelmanning.

✍️ In-class

🧪 One sentence, two halves in-class

Write a single sentence: "The strongest reason to take misalignment seriously is … and the strongest reason to doubt it is …" We collect these, surface the themes, and — importantly — revisit your own sentence in Session 20 to see whether the course changed your mind, and in which direction.

Counterpoint

• Hoes, E. & Gilardi, F. (2025), "Existential risk narratives about AI do not distract from its immediate harms" (PNAS, 122(16)).
