📈 Neural Scaling Laws

Why loss falls as a power law in compute, data and parameters — and why that's a safety fact

🎯 What we'll cover

One of the most consequential empirical findings in modern AI is also one of the simplest to state: make a transformer bigger, train it on more data with more compute, and its loss falls along a strikingly regular power law. This sub-session makes that precise and draws out why predictable capability growth is the engine behind the safety problem.

📉 The empirical law

Kaplan et al. (2020) found that test loss \(L\) scales as a power law in each of three quantities — model parameters \(N\), dataset size \(D\), and training compute \(C\) — over many orders of magnitude.

The shape

Holding the others non-bottlenecking, each relationship has the form

\[ L(N) \approx \left( \frac{N_c}{N} \right)^{\alpha_N} \]

a straight line on a log–log plot, with a small exponent (\(\alpha_N\) on the order of \(0.07\)). The remarkable facts are the regularity (clean power laws across ~7 orders of magnitude) and the smoothness (no plateau in sight over the studied range). Architecture details matter far less than scale.

🐭 Compute-optimal training: Chinchilla

⚖️ Spend a fixed compute budget well

If compute \(C\) is fixed (roughly \(C \approx 6ND\) for a dense transformer), how should you split it between a bigger model and more data? Hoffmann et al. (2022) — the "Chinchilla" paper — showed the field had been training models that were too large and too data-starved. For compute-optimal training, parameters and tokens should scale roughly in proportion. A smaller model trained on more data (Chinchilla) beat a much larger one (Gopher) at the same compute.

The practical upshot you'll meet all term: data, not just parameters, is a first-class scaling resource — and a binding constraint, since high-quality text is finite.

🛡️ Why scaling laws are a safety fact

Capabilities are forecastable

Loss — and, more loosely, many capabilities — can be predicted before a model is trained. Labs decide to build more dangerous systems partly because the payoff is foreseeable. Capability is on a planned trajectory.

Safety is not forecastable the same way

There is no clean scaling law for "won't deceive its overseer" or "is robust to jailbreaks". The quantity we can predict is the one we're least worried about; the ones we care about resist this kind of extrapolation.

Compute becomes the lever

If capability tracks compute, then compute is the thing you can forecast, meter, and govern (Sessions 4 and 12). Scaling laws are why "compute governance" is even coherent.

⚠️ What scaling laws do not say

They describe smooth falls in aggregate loss. They do not promise that every specific capability appears smoothly, nor that loss keeps falling forever, nor that "more scale" is the only path to capability. The relationship between a smoothly-falling loss and the sometimes-sudden appearance of specific skills is exactly the controversy we take up next.

📚 Readings

Core

• Kaplan, J., et al. (2020), "Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models" (arXiv:2001.08361).

• Hoffmann, J., et al. (2022), "Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models" (Chinchilla; arXiv:2203.15556).
