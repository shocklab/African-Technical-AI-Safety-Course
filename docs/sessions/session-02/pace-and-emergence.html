Pace of progress & the emergence debate

How fast is AI really improving — and do new abilities appear suddenly, or only seem to?

What we'll cover

Scaling laws describe a smooth fall in loss. But headlines describe abilities that appear "all at once". This sub-session separates what we can measure (compute trends) from what's contested (whether capabilities truly emerge), and uses a clean methodological dispute to practise the critical reading we set up in Session 1.

The measurable part: compute trends

What the data show

  • Training compute has grown enormously — for frontier models, several orders of magnitude over the last decade, far outpacing the historical hardware trend, driven by deliberate investment.
  • The three inputs scale together: compute, dataset size, and parameters — bounded increasingly by data and by energy/hardware (Session 4).
  • Trends are trackable: groups like Epoch AI maintain public datasets of model compute, parameters and data — the empirical basis for any honest claim about "the pace of AI".

You'll work with this data directly in the Session 2.5 lab. The discipline: when someone asserts AI is accelerating (or stalling), ask which measured quantity, over what window?

The contested part: do abilities "emerge"?

A genuine scientific dispute — and an instructive one, because both sides are looking at the same models.

The claim: emergence is real

Wei et al. (2022) documented "emergent abilities" — tasks (e.g. multi-step arithmetic) on which performance stays near chance until a scale threshold, then jumps sharply. If real, this is unsettling for safety: it means dangerous capabilities could appear without warning as models scale.

The rebuttal: a mirage?

Schaeffer, Miranda & Koyejo (2023) argued the sharp jumps are often artefacts of discontinuous metrics (exact-match accuracy). Switch to a smooth metric (e.g. per-token likelihood) and the same data show gradual, predictable improvement. On this view, "emergence" is partly in the ruler, not the model.

Why this dispute is the perfect Week-1 exercise

Notice what's going on: the disagreement is about the measurement, not the data. The same models look "suddenly capable" or "smoothly improving" depending on the metric you score them with — exactly the "what's the eval?" question from Session 1.4. The safety stakes are real either way: even if the underlying trend is smooth, the capability you care about (can it do the dangerous thing?) is naturally a thresholded, pass/fail quantity — so it can still surprise you in deployment.

Holding it honestly

The calibrated take

Aggregate progress is fast and, in loss terms, fairly predictable. Whether specific capabilities arrive smoothly or sharply is genuinely unsettled and metric-dependent — so confident predictions of either "imminent superintelligence" or "it's plateaued" are both overclaiming. For safety the prudent posture is the same under either reading: build the ability to measure capabilities and detect dangerous ones before deployment (Session 11), rather than trusting any extrapolation.

Readings

Core (read as a pair)

• Wei, J., et al. (2022), "Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models" (TMLR; arXiv:2206.07682).

• Schaeffer, R., Miranda, B. & Koyejo, S. (2023), "Are Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models a Mirage?" (NeurIPS 2023; arXiv:2304.15004).

Reference

• Epoch AI — public datasets and dashboards on training compute, parameters and data trends (epoch.ai).
