Week 2 • Session 4

⚡ The Physical Substrate of Scale

Compute, energy, water and critical minerals — and why the physical floor of AI is also its main governance lever

🎯 What we'll cover

Last session we stated the alignment problem in the abstract — proxies, optimisers, instrumental convergence. This session grounds the trajectory that makes alignment urgent in physical reality. Capability does not come from nowhere: it is bought with compute, and compute is bought with energy, water, hardware, and the minerals that hardware is made of.

We do three things. First, quantify the footprint of a frontier system — per query, per agentic task, and at deployment scale. Second, follow the supply chain from mine to data centre to e-waste, and confront the rebound problem. Third — and this is why it sits in a safety course, not only an ethics one — we show that because capability is gated on compute, compute is the most tractable thing we can actually govern. That argument reappears in Session 12.

🧭 Why a safety course counts kilowatts

Three reasons the physical substrate belongs in a technical AI safety course, not just a sustainability module.

Compute is the capability floor

Scaling laws (Session 2) make capability a fairly predictable function of compute. If you cannot get the compute, you cannot train the frontier model. That makes compute the one input in the AI value chain that is excludable, quantifiable, and detectable.

So compute is the governance lever

You cannot easily count algorithms or data, but you can count chips and measure power draw. Almost every concrete proposal for verifiable AI governance — export controls, compute thresholds, on-chip mechanisms — rests on this fact. The substrate is the policy surface.

The costs fall unevenly

The benefits of frontier AI concentrate where the capital and compute are; the energy, water and extraction costs often land elsewhere — disproportionately in the Global South. "Whose safety, whose cost?" is a question this course keeps asking.

📊 The cost of every prompt

Exact figures are uncertain — companies disclose little, so treat all numbers as order-of-magnitude guides. The shape of the picture is what matters.

💡 The transparency problem

Per-query energy and water figures are estimates built on partial disclosures, leaked specs, and modelling assumptions. Different credible sources disagree by an order of magnitude. The honest stance is to reason about ranges and trends, and to be sceptical of any single confident number — from advocates or critics alike.

Per interaction

A single short text exchange is small in isolation — comparable to a handful of web searches. The trap is reasoning from the single query: the footprint that matters is the aggregate across billions of daily interactions.

The agentic multiplier

An agent that plans, calls tools, reads results and re-queries can consume 10–50× the tokens of a one-shot answer; a "deep research" run can equal hundreds of standard queries. As the course moves toward agents (Weeks 5, 9), the energy cost compounds in ways invisible at the interface.

Water and heat

Data centres reject heat through evaporative cooling, consuming freshwater — a direct concern in water-stressed regions. The AU's own AI strategy flags data-centre cooling as a threat to regions "already facing water scarcity."

⛏️ The supply chain: minerals and embodied carbon

Before a single query runs, the hardware has already incurred a cost — in extraction and in manufacturing.

From mine to data centre to e-waste

  • Critical minerals: high-purity silicon, cobalt, lithium, copper, and rare-earth elements underpin every GPU and the systems that house them — geographically concentrated and geopolitically contested.
  • Embodied carbon: a large share of a chip's lifetime emissions is released during fabrication, before deployment. Most per-query estimates ignore it entirely.
  • End of line: rapid hardware refresh cycles generate e-waste, with environmental and human-health costs concentrated in specific regions.
  • The geopolitics: the same concentration that makes the supply chain fragile also makes it a lever — export controls on chips and fabrication equipment are already a front line of AI governance.

📈 Growth, efficiency, and the rebound problem

🔁 Jevons' paradox

Each model generation gets more efficient per unit of computation. Yet total energy use keeps climbing, because efficiency lowers cost, which expands deployment, which raises total consumption. Efficiency gains have so far been outpaced by growth in scale and usage.

For a safety course the lesson is structural, not moralising: do not assume that "the models will just get more efficient" solves the substrate problem. Absent a binding constraint, efficiency accelerates scale.

💡 The most impactful single lever (for your own work)

Where compute runs determines a large fraction of its carbon footprint — running an identical workload in a low-carbon grid region versus a high-carbon one can cut emissions dramatically, with zero change to the model or the result. When students run labs and projects this term (on Colab and beyond), region and model-size choices are the cheapest meaningful reductions available to them.

🌍 The African angle: grids, water, and sovereignty

This is a UCT course; the substrate question is not abstract here.

Carbon-intensive grids, water stress, and the compute gap

South Africa's grid has historically been among the world's most carbon-intensive (heavily coal-based), so AI workloads run locally inherit that intensity. Much of the continent faces water stress that data-centre cooling would aggravate. And the compute gap is stark: the chips, fabs, and hyperscale data centres that set the capability floor are overwhelmingly elsewhere.

This frames the sovereignty argument we return to in Session 12: if capability is gated on compute, and compute is concentrated, then "sovereign AI capacity" is first of all a question about access to the physical substrate — not slogans about models. The much-publicised continental AI-fund pledges are, so far, largely that — pledges — and still route through the same Northern hardware and data-centre stack.

🧭 From substrate to governance

The bridge to Week 6

Hold onto one chain of reasoning for Session 12: capability ← compute ← chips + energy. Each arrow is a place a governance regime could, in principle, intervene and verify — which is exactly why "compute governance" is one of the few proposals in technical AI governance with concrete, measurable hooks. The physical limits we counted today are the same limits that make some safety policies enforceable and others merely aspirational.

🧾 Summary & what's next

Capability is bought with compute; compute is bought with energy, water, hardware and minerals; and the resulting costs fall unevenly. Efficiency does not save us by default (Jevons). Crucially, because capability is gated on a countable, detectable input, the substrate is also the most governable point in the whole pipeline.
