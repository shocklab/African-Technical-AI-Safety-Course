Week 4 • Session 8

🪞 Scalable Oversight & "Whose Values?"

How do you supervise a system that may know more than you — and once you can, whose values should it be aligned to?

🎯 What we'll cover

Session 7 showed how labs scale feedback by replacing human raters with AI (RLAIF, Constitutional AI). That sharpens a problem rather than solving it. If a model becomes more capable than its supervisors on a task, how can a weaker overseer reliably train and check a stronger system? This is the scalable oversight problem, and it is partly technical and partly normative.

We cover the leading technical mechanisms — debate, recursive reward modelling, sandwiching, weak-to-strong generalisation — and then take the normative turn the engineering forces on us: alignment is always alignment to some values, so whose? Here the ethical frameworks earn their place in a technical course: the four philosophical lenses and Ubuntu/relational ethics are tools for reasoning about value specification, and preference aggregation runs straight into social-choice impossibility results — a result mathematicians will recognise.

🕳️ The supervision gap

The core difficulty in one sentence: training signals come from evaluators, and evaluators have limits.

💡 Why "just have humans check it" fails at the frontier

RLHF works when a human can judge which of two outputs is better. But for a long proof, a subtle security audit, or a 10,000-line refactor, the human may be unable to tell a correct answer from a confident wrong one. Worse, optimising against a flawed judge rewards persuasiveness over correctness — the Goodhart problem from Session 3, now aimed directly at the overseer. Scalable oversight asks how to amplify a limited overseer so the training signal stays tied to the truth.

🛠️ Mechanisms for amplifying oversight

Debate

Two strong models argue opposing answers in front of a weaker judge. The hope: it is harder to defend a falsehood than to refute it, so truth wins even when the judge could not have found it alone. The risk: debate may reward rhetorical skill, or both sides may collude.

Recursive reward modelling / IDA

Decompose a hard task into sub-tasks a human can judge, train assistants on those, and use them to help judge the harder whole. Oversight is built up recursively from pieces that remain checkable.

Sandwiching

An empirical test bed: put a model between non-expert overseers and ground-truth experts, and see whether non-experts plus the model can reach expert-level judgements. A way to study oversight today on tasks where we know the answer.

Weak-to-strong generalisation

Can a weak supervisor elicit the full capability of a strong model? Burns et al. fine-tune a strong model on a weak model's labels and find it can exceed its noisy supervisor — evidence, and a warning, about what supervision does and doesn't transfer.

⚠️ None of these is solved

Each mechanism has known failure modes (collusion, persuasive falsehoods, judges that anchor on fluency). Present them as the field's current best attempts, not settled methods. This is live research, and a fertile source of student projects (see Week 9).

⚖️ The normative turn: align to whose values?

Suppose oversight worked perfectly. You would still have to decide what you are overseeing toward. "Aligned" is not a property a model has on its own — it is a relation to a target, and the target is a values choice.

Four philosophical lenses on a single decision

A useful classroom tool (carried from the Generative-AI course): run any value-specification choice through four lenses and watch them disagree.

LensAsksWhere it bites in alignment
ConsequentialistWhat outcomes does this produce?Reward design; measuring helpfulness/harm by effects.
DeontologicalWhat rules/duties hold regardless of outcome?Hard constraints, "constitutions", refusal rules.
VirtueWhat would a good agent be like?Honesty, corrigibility framed as character, not just behaviour.
Relational / UbuntuWhat do our relationships and community require?Whose voices set the values; harms that are collective, not individual.

Ubuntu and relational ethics — not a footnote

Western alignment framings tend to start from an individual rational agent maximising a utility function. Sabelo Mhlambi's "From Rationality to Relationality" and Abeba Birhane's relational-ethics work argue that personhood and harm are relational — "a person is a person through other persons" — which reframes the alignment target itself. Whose preferences are even in the data? Whose harms are legible to the reward model? For a South African cohort this is the natural lens, and it directly motivates the Global-South safety thread in Session 18.

🧮 The maths hook: aggregating preferences

➗ "Whose values" is partly a theorem

If you try to align to everyone's values, you must aggregate conflicting preferences into one ordering. Social-choice theory says you cannot do this perfectly: Arrow's impossibility theorem shows no aggregation rule satisfies a short list of reasonable fairness conditions at once. RLHF's averaged reward model is silently making exactly such an aggregation — over whichever population the annotators happened to represent.

The takeaway is not nihilism but precision: "align to human values" hides a choice of whose, how weighted, and aggregated by what rule — and some of those choices are provably in tension.

🗣️ In-class: the debate exercise

🧪 Does debate actually help a weak judge? seminar

Two students argue opposite answers to a contested factual or value question; a third plays a deliberately under-informed "judge" and must rule using only the debate. Run it twice — once on a checkable factual question, once on a value-laden one.

Debrief: Did the judge converge on truth, or on the better rhetorician? What changed between the factual and the value question? Map the result back onto the AI-debate proposal and onto the social-choice problem above.

🧾 Summary & what's next

Scalable oversight is the attempt to keep a weak overseer's training signal tied to the truth as models outstrip us — via debate, recursive decomposition, sandwiching, and weak-to-strong generalisation, none yet solved. And even perfect oversight cannot escape the prior question of whose values the system targets, which is at once an ethical choice (four lenses, relational ethics) and a mathematical constraint (Arrow).

Next (Week 5, Sessions 9–10): robustness and the AI-control agenda — what happens when the system is adversarially attacked, or when we stop assuming it is even trying to cooperate.
