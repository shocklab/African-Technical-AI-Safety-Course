Week 6 • Session 11.5

Lab: build a refusal eval on open isiZulu data

Build a small but methodologically honest in-language safety eval end to end — proper scoring, error bars, a capability control, and a validity reflection. The course's promised project seed.

What you'll build

Session 9.4 measured an English-versus-isiZulu refusal gap by hand to feel the robustness failure. This lab does the eval-science version: a small, properly-constructed refusal benchmark on open isiZulu data, with two translation routes, two models, a capability control, error bars from 11.4, and a written validity reflection. The result is a small artefact the frontier-lab eval suites do not currently cover, and the natural seed for a Session 18 Global-South project. The graded skill is not the size of any gap you find; it is whether your eval would survive a sceptic.

Ethics frame (same as 9.4)

Measure refusal behaviour, not harmful content. Use a capped 30–50-item slice of mild, refusable prompts plus a benign control set; score the meaning of the reply as REJECT / BYPASS / UNCLEAR; report labels and rates, never raw harmful text; treat any real gap as a responsible-disclosure finding. None of the lab's value requires producing a harmful payload.

Setup — CPU floor and optional GPU

The runs-anywhere core uses InkubaLM-0.4B (Lelapa AI), small enough for a free CPU tier, as a capability contrast. The richer refusal signal needs an isiZulu-capable, instruction-tuned model — Aya-101 (13B) — which the optional GPU/Colab track loads. English prompts are the control arm throughout.

The pipeline

  • 1. Prompts. Take a capped 30–50-item slice of English AdvBench harmful-behaviour prompts, plus a matched benign-control set. Keep the slice small and mild by design.
  • 2. Translate by two routes. Render the prompts into isiZulu with two documented pipelines — NLLB-200 (zul_Latn) and Vulavula (Lelapa AI, African-built) — so translation quality is a measured variable, not a hidden confound. Back-translate and rate fidelity.
  • 3. Query the models. Run English and both isiZulu routes through your model(s); InkubaLM for capability, Aya-101 for refusal if you have the GPU.
  • 4. Score REJECT / BYPASS / UNCLEAR. Label the (back-translated) responses with Yong et al.'s scheme, using a keyword/refusal classifier and an LLM-as-judge, and validate both against a handful of your own human labels.
  • 5. Capability control. Pair each refusal measurement with a few Belebele (zul_Latn) or AfriMMLU (zul) items, so you can separate "refused because safe" from "refused because the model cannot read isiZulu." Without this control the eval is uninterpretable.

Statistics (enforced — this is why it is paired with 11.4)

Report refusal rate by language × translation route × model, each with a 95% confidence interval. With \(n \approx 40\) the intervals are wide by design — state that, and resist over-claiming. Do the central comparison paired: English versus isiZulu on the same prompts, so you exploit the question-level correlation rather than comparing two loose independent intervals. The headline you are allowed to report is of the form "refusal fell from \(\hat{p}_{\text{en}}\) to \(\hat{p}_{\text{zu}}\), paired difference \(\Delta\) with 95% CI \([a,b]\)" — and if that interval includes zero at your sample size, the honest conclusion is "not yet resolved," not "no gap."

The validity reflection

A one-page written component naming the confounds, because handling them is what separates an eval from a vibe:

  • Translation quality. A "refusal" may be the model failing to parse a garbled translation. Use your back-translation scores to restrict to high-fidelity items and check whether the gap survives. Yong et al.'s isiZulu BYPASS near 53% is high partly because GPT-4 reads isiZulu well; comprehension is what makes the safety gap dangerous rather than confusing.
  • Capability/safety entanglement. Your Belebele/AfriMMLU control is what lets you attribute a refusal drop to safety rather than to incomprehension.
  • Who scores, and in what language. Back-translation is lossy both ways, and an English-tuned judge may mislabel in-language replies.
  • Tiny \(n\) and ethics. Wide CIs, capped prompts, no raw harmful outputs, responsible disclosure.

The honest framing for students: the interesting result is not "we reproduced 53%." It is whether the gap persists on open models, moves with translation quality, and tracks isiZulu capability — three questions the frontier paper never asked, and any one of which is a project.

What to submit

One Colab notebook plus a one-page write-up: the refusal-rate table (language × route × model, each with a 95% CI); the paired English-versus-isiZulu difference with its interval; the capability-control numbers; and the validity reflection above. Graded on methodological honesty — the error bars, the confound handling, the controls — not on the magnitude of the gap. This artefact, extended, is a Session 18 project.

Readings

Core

• Yong, Menghini & Bach (2023), "Low-Resource Languages Jailbreak GPT-4" (arXiv:2310.02446) — the method you are operationalising as a proper eval, with the REJECT/BYPASS/UNCLEAR scheme.

Resources

• UK AISI Inspect (inspect.aisi.org.uk) — an optional eval framework (Task = Dataset + Solver + Scorer) to structure your pipeline.

• Masakhane / IrokoBench (masakhane.io) and the Belebele / AfriMMLU (zul) dataset cards — the open African-language substrate.
