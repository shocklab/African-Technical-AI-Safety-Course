The circuits paradigm: QK and OV

Reading a transformer as additive paths through a residual stream, and decomposing each attention head into where it attends and what it writes

What we'll cover

This is the algebra that makes mechanistic interpretability of transformers possible. We reframe the Session 2.2 forward pass in the interpretability idiom: the residual stream as a shared channel that every component reads from and writes to by addition, so that the output decomposes into a sum over independent paths. Then the central result: an attention head splits into a QK circuit that decides where it attends and an OV circuit that decides what it copies there, each a low-rank bilinear form. We derive both, show that only the matrix products matter (a gauge freedom), and see why one layer can only build lookup tables while two layers unlock composition. This is all reframing, not new architecture; Session 2.2 is assumed.

The residual stream as a communication channel

Every component reads a subspace, writes a subspace, and adds. Nothing overwrites; everything accumulates.

In a transformer, each attention head and MLP takes the current residual-stream vector as input and adds its output back:

\[ x_{\ell+1} \;=\; x_\ell \;+\; \sum_{h} \text{head}^{(h)}_\ell(x_\ell) \;+\; \text{MLP}_\ell(x_\ell). \]

Because contributions are added rather than overwritten, the residual stream behaves like a shared bus: a component "writes" information by adding a vector in some subspace, and a later component "reads" it by projecting onto that subspace. Unrolling the recursion, the final residual is the token embedding plus the sum of every component's contribution, and the logits are \(W_U\) applied to that sum. Setting aside LayerNorm (which is close to a fixed rescaling once trained), the whole network is therefore linear in the component outputs, and the logits decompose into a sum of terms — one per "path" through the network. This is the structural fact that licenses interpretability: you can ask what any single head contributes to the output, in isolation, because its contribution is an additive term you can read off.

Why "sum over paths" matters

If the model were a deep nest of compositions with no additive skeleton, isolating one head's effect would be hopeless. The residual stream's additivity is what makes "this head, via this matrix, did this" a meaningful sentence. Every technique in Sessions 14–15 — direct logit attribution, activation patching, ablation — is ultimately exploiting this decomposition.

An attention head splits into QK and OV

"Where to attend" and "what to write" are computed by two separate, independent low-rank maps.

A head has four learned matrices: \(W_Q, W_K, W_V\) (project the residual stream down to a small head dimension \(d_h\)) and \(W_O\) (project back up). The head does two things, and they factor apart.

The QK circuit — where it attends

The attention score between a query position \(i\) and a key position \(j\) is the dot product of their projected vectors:

\[ \text{score}(i,j) \;=\; (W_Q x_i)^\top (W_K x_j) \;=\; x_i^\top\, \underbrace{W_Q^\top W_K}_{\textstyle W_{QK}}\, x_j. \]

Define \(W_{QK} := W_Q^\top W_K\), a single \(d_\text{model}\times d_\text{model}\) bilinear form of rank at most \(d_h\). The softmax of these scores over \(j\) gives the attention pattern. So "where does position \(i\) look?" is governed entirely by the one matrix \(W_{QK}\) — a low-rank form that scores every (query, key) pair of residual-stream vectors.

The OV circuit — what it writes

Once the pattern \(a_{ij}\) is fixed, the head's contribution to position \(i\) is the attention-weighted sum of values, projected back up:

\[ \text{head}(x)_i \;=\; \sum_j a_{ij}\, W_O W_V\, x_j \;=\; \sum_j a_{ij}\, \underbrace{W_{OV}}_{\textstyle W_O W_V}\, x_j. \]

Define \(W_{OV} := W_O W_V\), again \(d_\text{model}\times d_\text{model}\) and rank \(\le d_h\). It says: for whatever token the head attends to, this is the transformation applied to its residual vector before it is written back. The attention pattern (from \(W_{QK}\)) and the written content (from \(W_{OV}\)) are completely separable — the head decides where to look and what to copy independently.

Only the products matter: a gauge freedom

The individual \(W_Q\) and \(W_K\) are not identifiable; only \(W_{QK}=W_Q^\top W_K\) is. For any invertible \(d_h\times d_h\) matrix \(R\), the reparameterisation \(W_Q \mapsto R^{-\top} W_Q,\ W_K \mapsto R\,W_K\) leaves the product unchanged:

\[ (R^{-\top}W_Q)^\top (R\,W_K) \;=\; W_Q^\top R^{-1} R\, W_K \;=\; W_Q^\top W_K. \]

The same holds for \(W_{OV}=W_O W_V\). The practical lesson: do not try to interpret \(W_Q\), \(W_K\), \(W_V\), \(W_O\) separately — they carry arbitrary internal coordinates. Interpret the gauge-invariant products \(W_{QK}\) and \(W_{OV}\). This is why the literature always talks about "the QK circuit" and "the OV circuit," never the four raw matrices.

One layer builds tables; two layers compute

A single attention-only layer can only implement lookup tables. Composition is what depth buys.

Consider a one-layer attention-only model (no MLP). Tracing the additive paths from embedding to unembedding, its logits decompose into exactly two readable contributions. The direct path (embedding straight to unembedding) implements a bigram table: which token tends to follow which, ignoring context. The attention path (through one head's OV, gated by its QK pattern) implements a skip-trigram table: "if the current token is \(B\) and there is an earlier token \(A\), boost token \(C\)" — a rule keyed on a pair of positions. Both are readable directly from the weight matrices \(W_{QK}\) and \(W_{OV}\) composed with embed/unembed. A one-layer model cannot do anything that requires using the output of one head as the input to another, because there is only one layer of heads. It is, in a precise sense, a pair of lookup tables.

Composition: what the second layer adds

With two layers, a later head can read what an earlier head wrote into the residual stream. There are three ways this happens, named by which input of the later head is affected:

  • Q-composition: the earlier head's output feeds the later head's query (changing where it looks based on prior computation).
  • K-composition: it feeds the later head's key (changing what counts as a match). This is the one induction heads use.
  • V-composition: it feeds the later head's value (changing what gets written).

In path terms, a two-layer model's logit decomposition now contains cross-terms in which one head's \(W_{OV}\) sits inside another head's \(W_{QK}\) or \(W_{OV}\). One such cross-term — a previous-token head's output entering a later head's key circuit — is exactly an induction head, the subject of 13.4. The algebra here makes that circuit visible before we name it: composition is the cross-paths that appear automatically once you expand the sum over two layers, not an extra mechanism added on top.

Questions to bring to class

  • Explain why the additivity of the residual stream is what makes "this head contributed this to the logits" a well-defined statement. What would break if components overwrote rather than added?
  • Derive the attention score as \(x_i^\top W_{QK} x_j\) and state the rank of \(W_{QK}\). Why is it low-rank, and why does that matter for interpretation?
  • Show that \(W_Q \mapsto R^{-\top}W_Q,\ W_K \mapsto R W_K\) leaves \(W_{QK}\) invariant. What practical mistake does this gauge freedom warn you against?
  • Why can a one-layer attention-only model implement only bigram and skip-trigram tables? What specific capability does the second layer's composition add?
