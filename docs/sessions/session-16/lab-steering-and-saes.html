Week 8 • Session 16.1

Lab: steering and interpreting SAE features

Build a steering vector by hand, then load a sparse autoencoder and interpret its features — and feel the 15.4 result for yourself

What you'll build

Two connected exercises. First you steer a model by adding a direction to its residual stream — the technique behind Golden Gate Claude (15.3), and the same kind of activation-space direction you met defensively in Session 10. Then you load a pretrained sparse autoencoder and interpret a handful of its features by hand, ablate one, and run the honesty check from 15.4: does an SAE-feature probe actually beat a plain logistic-regression probe? The steering track is the cheap warm-up; the SAE track is the main course. Everything has a CPU-floor version; the SAE-training stretch is optional GPU.

Track A (CPU floor) — activation steering

Load gpt2-small in TransformerLens on CPU. Build a steering vector as the difference of mean residual-stream activations between two small prompt sets — say "about the Golden Gate Bridge" minus neutral, or positive minus negative sentiment — at a chosen layer. Then add \(\alpha v\) to the residual stream during generation and sweep the strength \(\alpha\) and the layer. Watch the behaviour shift toward the concept, and watch it break into incoherence as \(\alpha\) grows. Open by recalling Session 10: there you saw activation-space directions used as a defence (representation rerouting / circuit breakers); here you build one as a technique, and see how blunt an instrument it is.

Track B (CPU-feasible, GPU-optional) — interpret SAE features

CPU floor: load a pretrained gpt2-small SAE via SAELens. Run text through it, find which features fire, and interpret three to five by hand: collect each feature's top-activating contexts and write a one-line monosemanticity verdict (is it really one concept?). Then ablate one feature (zero its activation) and observe the effect on the model's logits — e.g. a punctuation feature that, removed, changes the next-token distribution in a readable way. GPU stretch (optional, ~1 GPU-hour on the cluster): train a small SAE on one layer of Pythia-70M's residual stream, sweep the sparsity \(k\), plot the reconstruction-versus-sparsity Pareto curve from 15.3, and note how many latents come out dead.

The honesty check (do this — it is the point of 15.4)

Pick one simple binary concept with a few dozen labelled examples. Train two probes: a logistic regression on the raw residual stream, and a probe on the SAE features. Compare held-out accuracy with the error bars from Session 11.4. If the SAE probe does not clearly beat the raw-activation baseline, you have reproduced the 2025 reassessment by hand — and learned to distrust any "SAEs help" claim that did not run this comparison. Resist the weak-baseline temptation: give the logistic regression a fair pooling of the activations.

What to submit

One Colab notebook plus a short write-up: the steering sweep (a few outputs across \(\alpha\) and layer, with a sentence on where it breaks); a feature dossier (3–5 SAE features with top-activating contexts, a monosemanticity verdict each, and one ablation result); and the honesty-check numbers (raw-activation probe vs SAE-feature probe, with confidence intervals) plus a sentence on which won and what that implies. Graded on the quality of the interpretation and the fairness of the baseline, not on finding a flashy feature.
