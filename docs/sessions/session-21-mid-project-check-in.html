Week 11 • Session 21

Mid-project check-in

A structured progress review, and a decision: push on, pivot, or cut

The mid-project review

The halfway mark. The purpose of a mid-project review is to surface a stuck project while there is still time to rescue it, and to give every team permission to change course before the cost of finishing the wrong thing gets too high. Each team takes the review below, out loud, in front of the group. It is short and a little uncomfortable by design.

Where you should be by now

After the clinic in Session 19, the bar is concrete: a first result and the baseline that tests it.

You do not need a finished result at this point, but you do need a real one: a first version of your single figure, and the baseline that could explain it away. A team that still has no measurement is not behind on polish; it is behind on the thing that decides whether the project works, and that is the situation this session exists to catch. If that is you, say so first, because it is the most useful thing to put on the table.

The three-question review

Every team answers these three, briefly, with evidence rather than intention.

  • What is your one figure, and do you have a draft of it? Show it, even if it is ugly. A drawn-by-hand sketch of the figure you are aiming for is fine; a paragraph describing what you hope to find is not.
  • What does your baseline say? If the simple baseline already explains your effect, that is the most important thing you have learned, and the review is the place to confront it.
  • What is the single biggest risk to finishing? Name the one thing most likely to stop you, concretely enough that the group can help.

Push, pivot, or cut

The honest decision the review forces. Sunk cost is not a reason to continue.

Three outcomes

Push if the core measurement works and the result is taking shape: keep going, tighten the experiment, start writing. Pivot if the question is sound but the method is stuck: keep the question, change the approach (a different model, a smaller setting, a cheaper proxy). Cut if neither is working: reduce to the smaller, honest result you can actually deliver. A clearly-reported negative result on a real question ("we tried X to close the multilingual gap and it did not work, here is the evidence") is a perfectly good project, and it scores on the same rubric as a positive one. The failure to avoid is not a negative result; it is no result, because you spent the time defending a plan instead of testing it.

Common failure modes at the midpoint

  • The dramatic result that is a bug. A too-good first number is usually a leak or an off-by-one. Try to break it before the review, not after.
  • Scope crept back up. Teams quietly re-add the ambitions they cut in Session 17. Re-apply the test: name the single figure.
  • Divided work that does not integrate. Two members built two halves that do not connect. Integrate early; a working whole beats two polished halves.
  • Waiting for the perfect run. Endless tuning in place of writing. Once the figure exists, start the paper; the writing exposes what is missing.

Start writing now

The write-up is not the last step; it is a tool for finding the holes while you can still fill them.

By the end of this week you should have a draft abstract and a draft of your one figure. Writing the abstract forces you to state your claim in a sentence, and that sentence usually reveals whether you have a result or only an activity. The limitations section is where the pivots and cuts from this review live honestly: what you tried, what did not work, and what your result does not cover. None of that loses marks; the rubric rewards it.

Two things to bring

Two things: a draft of your single figure, and your push/pivot/cut decision with the reason behind it. If the reason is "we are not sure yet", the review is where we work out the decision together.

Questions to answer in the review

  • What is your one figure, and what does the current draft of it show?
  • Has your baseline survived contact with your result, or explained it away?
  • Push, pivot, or cut: what is the evidence for that choice?
  • What will you have written by the end of the week?
